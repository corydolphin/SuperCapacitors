The power grid

The grid is what we call the energy distribution system that moves electricity from the power plant to other buildings. Typically, the power plant uses an engine to spin a turbine. That spinning creates an alternating electrical current, which can be then converted to extremely high voltage- around 100,000 Volts. That high voltage travels happily along power lines until it reaches a neighborhood transformer, which pulls the voltage back down to the friendly 120- and 240- Volts we get from our plugs. That method lets power plants reach a wide distance, since high voltage can transmit a lot of energy.

Most of the power in the United States comes from coal and natural gas. These non-renewable resources are cheap and plentiful, but they produce carbon dioxide, are contributors of pollution, and will not last forever.

About a third of the nation's energy comes from alternative sources - nuclear, hydroelectric, and things like wind and solar. Those have their own issues, but advances in technology could make them more viable.
